_Originally Preached at St. Stephen's Episcopal Church and University Center in Columbus, Ohio on July 5, 2020_

[In our Gospel for today](http://bible.oremus.org/?ql=462896286), Jesus comes right out of the gate with a challenge to those listening. He takes them to task for their response to his ministry announcing the peaceful reign of God and the goodness of that reign. Expecting a great conquering king who would liberate them from the oppressive rule of the Roman Empire, Jesus’ talk of the coming peaceful reign of God seemed absurd, and not nearly spiritual enough.

On the other hand, the crowd is rebuked also for rejecting John the Baptist’s ministry, depicted here as a funeral, filled with wailing and mourning. John’s ministry called the residents of Judea to repentance and warned them of the coming of the Messiah. For the religious elites, who surely would have been listening, this was too much. It threatened their authority and his vocation of lamentation in the desert was too unworldly for them to stomach.

The discomfort privileged and ruling classes feel toward calls to repentance isn’t just present in Jesus’ time but is more than present in our lives even now. Just this week, images of a New York City street circulated on social media. In the foreground were brunch-goers sitting on a restaurant patio. In the background were protestors, fighting to bring the murderers of George Floyd and Breonna Taylor and Ahmaud Arbery and Tony McDade and Rayshard Brooks to justice.

[In another video](https://twitter.com/XX_MORENA/status/1277614417472040961?s=20) posted to Twitter this week, protestors march through the Flatbush neighborhood of Brooklyn, with one side of the street filled with upscale apartment buildings and the other cafes, an area nearly indistinguishable from Columbus’ own Short North, Old Towne East, or increasingly Franklinton. The protestors chanted “Black people used to live here…fire, fire gentrifier” while the uncomfortable-looking brunch-goers looked on.

In both of these clips, we see the legacy of John’s prophetic truth-telling in action. The juxtaposition of majority Black protestors against white brunch-goers in the midst of a pandemic is telling. The discomfort in the faces of those on-lookers resembles the crowds Jesus spoke to in our Gospel today.

Our discomfort with truth-telling and lamentation goes as deeply as the verse selections made by the scholars responsible for putting together the Revised Common Lectionary, which we use every Sunday in worship. Verses 20–24 are missing from the lectionary entirely, perhaps reflecting the difficulty of these verses. In them, Jesus declares woes upon Jewish cities who have experienced his deeds of power and yet have failed to repent. Reflecting the book of Jonah, and turning the Old Testament genre of “woes against foreign nations” on its head, Jesus points out that “If the deeds of power done in [Chorazin and Bethsaida] had been done in Tyre and Sidon (majority Gentile cities), they would have repented long ago in sackcloth and ashes.”

This reading calls on us to do the difficult and important work of self-examination and penance for our sins. And the sins of our public life could not be more clear today, the day after Independence Day. Countless sermons could be preached and adult education classes taught on the roles that racism and colonialism have played in making The Episcopal Church into what it is today. We could spend hours discussing how our cathedrals were built using monies earned from the enslavement of human beings, or how our need and desire to be close to power has led to the defilement of our sacred spaces and the tear-gassing of our priests and seminarians. But our Gospel for today calls us into deeper repentance of societal ills, and self-examination for how the sins of our nation and society have embedded themselves in our psyche.

Many of us will be appalled by this suggestion. “But Sam,” we say to ourselves. “I’m not racist!” And while that may be true, being simply “not racist” still allows us to participate in systems of oppression in every aspect of our lives. It is not enough to be not racist. We must be unapologetically anti-racist.
Anti-racism is not a one and done sort of thing. Anti-racism is a grueling slog through deeply internalized behaviors that touch every part of our lives. Doing the hard work of anti-racism requires us to question our beliefs on punishment. It requires us to interrogate why we feel discomfort when someone suggests abolishing the police or abolishing prisons. We must ask ourselves, why do we insist on the continued policing of majority-minority communities when white-majority communities do not experience the same levels of policing, and yet have lower crime rates? Is it because we think that broken windows policing and stop-and-frisk have an effect on crime? Or is it because we cannot bear to imagine ourselves in a world where our black and brown siblings are not being policed?
If I asked you all what your position on slavery would have been if you lived in the 1830s, I think the majority of you would give me some variety of “I would have been an abolitionist and fought to spread the good news.”

I’m sorry to say you are more than likely wrong.

Helen Andrews, the editor of the American Conservative offered this thought experiment on Twitter this week. She writes:
> "The best thought experiment for how you would have approached slavery if you were born in 1820 is to imagine talking to someone from a civilization that has abolished wage labor and who regards the employment relationship as fundamentally exploitative and evil."




How easily we make excuses when we turn our attention from slavery to wage labor! We say things like “Well, some bosses are nice!” or “No one today thinks wage labor is evil. It’s not even debated.” When we go through this framework, we seem like disgruntled radicals who are ostracized from society, just as slavery abolitionists were seen as disgruntled radicals.

In her book _Are Prisons Obsolete_, American political philosopher and activist Angela Davis writes:
>“On the whole, people tend to take prisons for granted. It is difficult to imagine life without them. At the same time, there is reluctance to face the realities hidden within them, a fear of thinking about what happens in them…..We Take prisons for granted but are often afraid to face the realities they produce. After all, no one wants to go to prison. Because it would be too agonizing to cope with the possibility that anyone, including ourselves, could become a prisoner, we tend to think of prison as disconnected from our own lives….We thus think about imprisonment as a fate reserved for others…. The prison therefore functions ideologically as an abstract site into which undesirables are deposited, relieving us of the responsibility of thinking about the real issues afflicting those communities from which prisoners are drawn in such disproportionate numbers.”

It is easy for us to sink back into our old patterns of discomfort. We dismiss proposals for the abolition of police out of hand for being too idealistic, for not being rooted in reality. We counter arguments for the abolition of prisons by holding tightly onto our punitive understanding of punishment —-- arguing for an eye for an eye and a tooth for a tooth while denying the real underlying issues that lead to people being in situations where they commit a crime. We ignore racial biases in hiring that cause brown and black folks to be passed over for promotions. We ignore medical racism that causes medical bills to mount as doctors and nurses ignore symptoms or think black and brown patients are faking it. We ignore the lack of a social framework for people reentering society after serving time in prison, leading to high rates of re-offending.

When Jesus of Nazareth comes again in glory to establish the New Heaven and the New Earth, there will be no police, no military, no prisons. All earthly employment shall pass away and all our needs will be satisfied. Every person will have a home, and food enough to eat. Those underlying conditions will be healed, and we will live in justice and peace with one another. When we pray in just a few minutes “thy kingdom come, thy will be done on earth as it is in heaven,” we tie ourselves to that eschatological vision.

In the latter verses of our Gospel for today, Jesus tells us to take on his yoke — to take on the teachings of justice and compassion that undergird the Gospel and work to bring the Kingdom here on Earth. Jesus is saying “I know what I am asking you is difficult, but if you trust in me and believe, you will find a person to lean on when the going gets tough.”

The work of antiracism is difficult. You will lose friends and family. People who you have been close to for your entire life will abandon you because you threaten their comfortable way of life. Just two weeks ago we heard Jesus’ difficult words that he came to set families and friends against one another. We heard his call to take up our crosses and follow him. That call echos today. For many of you, that call is uncomfortable, it is perhaps even terrifying. But when that work is done in Christian community, among people who love and care and support you, you will find that “the yoke is easy, and the burden is light.”

__Amen.__
